Context

The Challenge

We partnered with the Responsible Technology & Society Lab, a research group studying how AI, automation, and digital systems are reshaping society. Their work was solid, but their website didn't show it. There was no real visual identity, and the site itself looked like it hadn't changed in years. People had a hard time finding them, and when they did, nothing on the page told them to take the research seriously.

Client Brief

What RTS Lab Asked For

The client didn't want a logo refresh. They wanted a full brand and digital experience, one that could speak to researchers, students, industry partners, and policymakers all at once.

01

A direction we could actually stand behind, something that didn't blend into every other research lab's branding.

02

Human + Technology

A visual language that makes "responsible tech" feel like something people can relate to, not just a research topic.

03

Inclusive, Global Reach

Imagery that speaks to the people actually affected by these systems, not only the researchers studying them.

04

A System That Scales

A logo, brand guidelines, real UX research, and a working website prototype for desktop and mobile.

Discovery

How We Started

Before we opened Illustrator, we looked at five similar research labs. Most of them felt cold, or just visually inconsistent. None of them felt warm and organized at the same time, so that's what we decided to go after.

"For researchers, students, and policy-facing partners, the RTS Lab makes complex technological systems more understandable, ethical, and socially relevant."
Identity

Finding Loop Layers

Everyone on the team brought three concepts of their own to critique. Instead of picking one winner, we pulled pieces from three different directions and merged them into Loop Layers, the one that actually shipped.

Reflection

What this project taught me

On this project I wasn't just designing, I was also acting as project manager, so I was juggling both roles at once. That combination taught me how much communication actually matters. With five people pulling from different concepts and different schedules, it was easy for everyone to drift into their own version of the idea. Keeping the team on the same page, through regular check-ins, clear feedback, and just over-communicating decisions, ended up mattering as much as any single design choice.

If I did this again, I'd set up that communication rhythm earlier, before the first critique, instead of figuring it out as we went.
